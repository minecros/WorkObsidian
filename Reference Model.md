Provides the overall conceptual basis for the more detailed models to follow.
The **ISA99 Reference Model** provides a **layered view of how systems interact**.
At **Level 0**, we find the **physical process** — this includes machinery, sensors, valves, and actuators that **directly perform or monitor tasks** like mixing chemicals, moving conveyor belts, or regulating temperatures.
Just above that, at **Level 1**, are the **basic control devices** such as Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs) and Remote Terminal Units (RTUs). These smart devices **interpret data** from sensors and **issue commands** to actuators, **automating key control functions**.
Moving up to **Level 2**, we find **supervisory control**, where systems like Human-Machine Interfaces (HMIs) and SCADA platforms allow operators to **monitor** real-time data, **make adjustments**, and **visualize process status** across the facility.
At **Level 3**, the focus shifts to **operations management**—systems like Manufacturing Execution Systems (MES) coordinate production schedules, track product quality, and ensure workflows are optimized and aligned with business goals.
Above that, **Level 4** includes the **business logistics systems**. Enterprise IT systems, such as Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) **manage corporate tasks** like inventory, finance, and supply chain logistics.

Each **level has a specific role and set of risks**, and understanding them helps organizations implement layered, **defense-in-depth** **cybersecurity** strategies.
- Level 0: Physical Process - This is the real-world environment where the actual industrial process occurs. Examples include sensors, actuators, motors, and valves.
- Level 1: Basic Control - Devices that directly monitor and control level 0. Examples include PLCs, RTUs, and embedded controllers. 
- Level 2: Area Supervisory Control - Systems for monitoring and controlling the process at an area or unit level. Examples include HMIs, SCADA, and DCS interfaces
- Level 3: Operations Management - System that coordinate the production workflow, performance monitoring, and quality control. Examples include MES(Manufacturing Execution Systems) and batch management software.
- Level 4: Business Logistics Systems - Enterprise-level systems for business planning and logistics. This level is managed by IT, not OT. Examples include ERP(Enterprise Resource Planning), CRM, and a financial systems.
